No, Getting a Second Citizenship Doesn't Mean Giving Up Your First: 7 Myths Debunked

July 17, 2026 | Meridian Advisory

By Meridian Advisory | Updated January 2026

It's the single most common misconception we hear in our consultations. Week after week, highly successful, deeply intelligent entrepreneurs and investors sit across from our senior advisor Rachel and ask the same question:

"If I get a second citizenship, do I have to give up my current one?"

The answer, in the vast majority of cases, is a resounding no.

Yet this myth persists — and it's costing people years of inaction on a decision that could transform their global mobility, tax strategy, and family legacy. Let's dismantle this misconception and six others that keep high-net-worth individuals on the sidelines.

Myth #1: "Second Citizenship Means Renouncing Your First"

The Reality: The overwhelming majority of countries in the world permit dual (or even multiple) citizenship. The United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, France, Germany, Portugal, and dozens of other nations have no issue with their citizens holding a second passport.

Citizenship by Investment (CBI) programs — like those in St. Kitts & Nevis, Grenada, Malta, and Portugal — are specifically designed to add a citizenship. They don't require you to surrender anything.

Here's what's actually happening when you obtain a second citizenship through investment:

  • ✅ You retain your original nationality
  • ✅ You gain an additional passport and all associated rights
  • ✅ Your children can often inherit both citizenships
  • ✅ You are not reported to your home country unless specific treaties apply

The confusion often stems from a handful of countries that do restrict dual citizenship — such as China, India, the UAE, and Singapore. But even citizens of these nations have legal strategies worth exploring. The point is: the rule is permission, not prohibition.

Myth #2: "It's Only for People Trying to Dodge Taxes"

The Reality: This myth says more about Hollywood stereotypes than about the actual CBI client profile. In 2026, the people pursuing second citizenship are:

  • Tech founders who want visa-free access to 150+ countries without applying for visas weeks in advance
  • Parents who want their children to have the option of studying and working in the EU or the Caribbean without immigration hurdles
  • Investors diversifying geopolitical risk the same way they diversify an investment portfolio
  • Retirees seeking better healthcare, lower cost of living, or simply a warm place to spend six months a year — legally and comfortably

Is tax optimization part of the conversation? Sometimes. But it's one factor among many, and any legitimate advisory firm — including ours — works within the full bounds of international tax law. The era of shadowy offshore schemes is over. Modern CBI is transparent, regulated, and fully compliant.

Myth #3: "My Home Country Will Find Out and There Will Be Consequences"

The Reality: Most countries simply don't care. Dual citizenship is a legal status recognized under international law. You aren't doing anything covert.

That said, some countries do have reporting requirements. For example:

  • U.S. citizens must continue filing U.S. taxes regardless of where they live or what other passports they hold. But holding a second passport is perfectly legal.
  • Some European nations require you to notify authorities of a new citizenship. Notification, not permission.

The key distinction: notification is not the same as prohibition. Knowing your specific obligations based on your current nationality is exactly what a proper consultation covers.

Myth #4: "CBI Passports Are 'Second-Class' Passports"

The Reality: A passport from St. Kitts & Nevis provides visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to over 155 countries, including the entire EU Schengen Area, the United Kingdom, Singapore, and Hong Kong. Grenada's passport — notably — includes access to China via a unique visa waiver, plus eligibility for the U.S. E-2 Treaty Investor Visa, a pathway unavailable to citizens of many larger nations.

Malta? It's an EU passport. Full stop. That means the right to live, work, and access healthcare across 27 EU member states.

These aren't consolation prizes. These are some of the most strategically powerful travel documents in the world — and they carry the full legal weight of national citizenship.

Myth #5: "The Process Takes Years and Is Incredibly Complicated"

The Reality: Timelines vary by program, but here's what 2026 actually looks like:

| Program | Typical Timeline | Minimum Investment |

|---|---|---|

| St. Kitts & Nevis | 3–6 months | ~$250,000 (donation) |

| Grenada | 4–6 months | ~$235,000 (donation) |

| Portugal Golden Visa | 12–18 months (residency) | ~€500,000 (fund investment) |

| Malta Exceptional Investor Naturalization | 12–36 months | €690,000+ (combined) |

St. Kitts even offers an Accelerated Application Process that can deliver citizenship in as few as 45–60 days.

Is there paperwork? Yes. Due diligence? Absolutely — and that's a feature, not a bug. Rigorous vetting protects the integrity of the programs and the value of the passport you receive. But "complicated"? With the right advisory team, the process is methodical, well-mapped, and far smoother than most people expect.

Myth #6: "It's Not Worth It Unless You Plan to Actually Move There"

The Reality: The vast majority of CBI citizens never relocate to their second country of citizenship. And that's perfectly fine — it's by design.

Programs like St. Kitts & Nevis and Grenada have zero residency requirements. You don't need to visit. You don't need to live there. You receive a passport, and you use it as a strategic tool:

  • Travel to countries where your primary passport faces restrictions
  • Open bank accounts and investment vehicles in new jurisdictions
  • Provide a safety net for your family in case of political instability, conflict, or economic crisis at home
  • Access healthcare and education systems in new regions

Think of it as an insurance policy. You hope you never need to use it urgently — but you sleep far better knowing it's there.

Myth #7: "This Is Only for Billionaires"

The Reality: Some programs start at investment levels comparable to a modest real estate purchase. A $250,000 donation to the St. Kitts Sustainable Island State Contribution (SISC) fund can secure citizenship for an individual. Family applications are available at scaled pricing.

To put that in context: it's less than the median home price in most major U.S. and European cities.

Are there programs at the higher end? Absolutely — Malta's program, with its combined contributions exceeding €690,000, is positioned for ultra-high-net-worth individuals. But the ecosystem as a whole is accessible to a far broader range of successful professionals than most people realize.

If you own a business, hold meaningful investments, or have built a career that affords you a solid six- or seven-figure net worth, second citizenship is likely within your reach.

The Real Risk Isn't Getting a Second Citizenship — It's Waiting

In 2026, the landscape is shifting. Programs evolve. Prices increase. Due diligence standards tighten. Countries that offer CBI today may change their terms tomorrow — we've already seen it with Dominica's program adjustments, EU pressure on certain Golden Visa schemes, and the ongoing evolution of international tax transparency frameworks like CRS.

The window is open now. It won't stay open forever, and it certainly won't get cheaper.
