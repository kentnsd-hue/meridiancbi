The Cost of Waiting: Why Every Year You Delay CBI, You Pay More

June 09, 2026 | Meridian Advisory

How rising investment thresholds, closing programs, and tighthat regulatory windows are making second citizenship more expensive with each passing quarter.

Published by Meridian Advisory | June 2026

The Uncomfortable Truth About "I'll Do It Next Year"

If you've been researching Citizenship by Investment for a while — bookmarking articles, saving program comparisons, telling yourself you'll "look into it properly next quarter" — this post is for you.

Because the data tells a very clear story: CBI programs do not get cheaper over time. They get more expensive, more restrictive, or they disappear entirely.

And 2026 is proving that pattern holds.

A Decade of Rising Thresholds: The Numbers Don't Lie

Let's look at actual price movements across the most popular CBI programs over the past several years.

🇰🇳 St. Kitts & Nevis

The world's longest-running CBI program has undergone multiple price adjustments:

| Year | Minimum Donation (Single Applicant) | Change |

|------|--------------------------------------|--------|

| 2018 | $150,000 | — |

| 2023 | $250,000 (SIDF restructured) | +67% |

| 2024 | Pricing held, but due diligence fees increased | +fees |

| 2026 | Enhanced due diligence surcharges and processing fees now push total costs well above $300,000 for families | +100%+ from 2018 |

A family of four that could have secured citizenship for roughly $200,000 in 2018 is now looking at $350,000–$400,000+ for the same outcome.

That's not inflation. That's a fundamentally different price point.

🇬🇩 Grenada

Grenada's program — the only Caribbean CBI that includes access to the U.S. E-2 Treaty Investor visa — has seen similar trends:

  • 2019: $150,000 minimum donation for a single applicant
  • 2023: $235,000 following the Caribbean-wide harmonization agreement
  • 2024–2026: Additional government fees, compliance levies, and extended processing timelines have driven effective costs higher still

The E-2 treaty access alone makes Grenada extraordinarily valuable. And as demand grows from U.S.-adjacent entrepreneurs, there is zero incentive for the government to lower costs.

🇵🇹 Portugal Golden Visa

This is perhaps the most dramatic cautionary tale:

  • Pre-2022: You could invest €280,000 in a property renovation in a low-density area and qualify.
  • 2023: Real estate investment was removed entirely as a qualifying route.
  • 2024–2026: Remaining fund-based investment options start at €500,000, with increased regulatory scrutiny, longer processing, and talk of further program modifications under EU pressure.

Investors who acted in 2021 locked in a real estate path that no longer exists at any price. Those who waited didn't just pay more — they lost access to the most attractive option permanently.

🇲🇹 Malta

Malta's Exceptional Investor Naturalization (MEIN) policy remains one of the only pathways to EU citizenship by investment. It's also one of the most expensive:

  • Minimum contribution: €600,000+ (with a 36-month residency) or €750,000 (12-month residency)
  • Real estate and donation requirements on top
  • Total family cost regularly exceeds €1 million

And here's the critical context: The EU has been actively pressuring member states to close CBI programs entirely. Malta's program faces ongoing legal challenges from the European Commission. There is a real, non-trivial chance this pathway narrows further or closes within the next few years.

Why Do CBI Programs Only Get More Expensive?

This isn't random. There are structural reasons prices move in one direction.

1. Demand Is Accelerating

Global instability, tax policy shifts, remote work normalization, and geopolitical fragmentation are driving unprecedented demand for second citizenship. The 2024–2026 period has seen record application volumes across nearly every major program.

When demand rises and supply is controlled by sovereign governments, prices go up.

2. Due Diligence Costs Are Rising

Post-pandemic, international compliance standards have tightened considerably. Programs now conduct deeper background checks, engage more third-party verification firms, and spend more per applicant on screening. Those costs are passed directly to you.

3. International Regulatory Pressure

The EU, OECD, and FATF continue to scrutinize CBI programs. Governments respond by raising standards — and prices — to demonstrate program integrity. The Caribbean harmonization agreement of 2023, which set a $200,000 minimum floor across the region, is a direct example.

4. Programs Close Without Warning

Consider these losses over the past few years:

  • Cyprus shut down its CBI program in 2020 following corruption scandals — overnight, a path to EU citizenship vanished
  • Portugal eliminated its real estate route in 2023
  • Bulgaria closed its investor immigration program
  • Ireland ended its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023

No one got advance notice to "lock in" their application. The window simply closed.

The Hidden Costs of Delay (Beyond the Sticker Price)

Even if program fees stayed flat — which they won't — waiting creates costs that most people don't calculate:

💰 Opportunity Cost of Tax Planning

Every year without an optimized tax residency strategy is a year of potentially overpaying. For high-income entrepreneurs, the delta can be six or seven figures annually.

✈️ Mobility Cost

Travel restrictions, visa requirements, and geopolitical disruptions are increasing. A second passport today isn't a luxury — it's operational infrastructure for anyone running a global business.

⏱️ Processing Timelines Are Lengthening

Higher application volumes mean longer queues. Programs that processed in 60 days in 2021 may now take 6–9 months. Starting your application today in mid-2026 means you could have citizenship by early 2027. Wait another year, and that timeline may stretch to late 2028.

🏛️ Regulatory Risk

You are not just racing against prices. You're racing against the possibility that your preferred program fundamentally changes its terms, raises its minimum investment again, or ceases to exist.

The Psychology of "Later" (And Why It's Your Biggest Risk)

We see it constantly at Meridian Advisory. Highly sophisticated, data-driven individuals — people who would never delay a business decision with this risk profile — somehow convince themselves that second citizenship can wait.

The usual refrains:

> "I'll revisit this in Q4."

> "I want to see what happens with [election / regulation / market]."

> "It's on my list for next year."

Meanwhile, the programs they're eyeing raise fees, add restrictions, or close entirely.

Every CBI applicant we work with wishes they had started sooner. Not one has wished they had waited.

What Acting Now Actually Looks Like

Pursuing CBI isn't an overnight decision, and no ethical advisor would tell you otherwise. But there's a massive difference between actively evaluating your options with an expert and passively bookmarking articles for another six months.

Here's what a smart first step looks like:

1. A 30-minute consultation to assess your specific situation — nationality, family structure, business interests, tax residency, travel patterns

2. A tailored comparison of 2–3 programs that align with your goals and budget

3. A clear timeline and cost breakdown so you can make a decision based on facts, not assumptions

That's it. No commitment. No pressure. Just clarity.

The Bottom Line

The historical trend is unambiguous:

  • CBI investment minimums have increased 50–100%+ over the past 5–7 years
  • Multiple programs have closed permanently with no warning
  • Processing times are lengthening as demand grows
  • Regulatory pressure makes further restrictions likely, not just possible

The best time to apply was five years ago. The second-best time is now — before the programs you're considering today become the cautionary tales of 2028.
