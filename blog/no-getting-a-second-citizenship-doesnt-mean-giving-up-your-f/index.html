No, Getting a Second Citizenship Doesn't Mean Giving Up Your First: 7 Myths We Hear Every Week

May 10, 2026 | Meridian Advisory

Published by Meridian Advisory | June 2026

It's the single most common misconception we encounter at Meridian Advisory — and in 2026, it's still stopping otherwise brilliant, globally minded individuals from taking action on one of the most powerful strategic assets available to them.

"If I get a second citizenship, I'll have to give up my first one."

This is, in the vast majority of cases, simply not true.

Yet the myth persists. It circulates in WhatsApp groups, gets repeated at dinner parties, and quietly discourages thousands of entrepreneurs, investors, and families from even exploring their options. The result? Missed opportunities for global mobility, tax efficiency, generational wealth protection, and genuine peace of mind.

Let's dismantle this — myth by myth, fact by fact.

Myth #1: "Most Countries Don't Allow Dual Citizenship"

Reality: The overwhelming majority of countries do.

As of 2026, more than 75% of nations worldwide permit some form of dual or multiple citizenship. This includes major economies and passport powerhouses like:

  • 🇺🇸 United States
  • 🇬🇧 United Kingdom
  • 🇨🇦 Canada
  • 🇫🇷 France
  • 🇦🇺 Australia
  • 🇵🇹 Portugal
  • 🇮🇹 Italy
  • 🇧🇷 Brazil

The global trend has been moving decisively toward acceptance of multiple nationalities, not away from it. Countries recognize that in a deeply interconnected world, forcing citizens to choose a single allegiance is both impractical and counterproductive to attracting global talent and investment.

Are there exceptions? Yes. A small number of countries — including China, India, the UAE (in most cases), and Singapore — do not formally recognize dual citizenship or require renunciation upon naturalization elsewhere. But here's the critical nuance: obtaining a second citizenship through a Citizenship by Investment (CBI) program does not automatically trigger renunciation of your first. The restriction, where it exists, typically applies in the other direction — if you were to naturalize in one of those restrictive countries, they might require you to give up a previous nationality.

If you hold a passport from any of the countries on the "permissive" list — which, again, is most of them — acquiring a second citizenship from St. Kitts & Nevis, Grenada, Malta, or through Portugal's Golden Visa pathway changes absolutely nothing about your original nationality.

Myth #2: "My Government Will Find Out and Revoke My Passport"

Reality: Acquiring a second citizenship is legal, and democratic governments don't revoke passports for it.

This fear often stems from a general anxiety about government overreach rather than any grounded legal reality. In countries that permit dual citizenship — the US, UK, Canada, most of the EU — there is no legal mechanism to revoke your citizenship simply because you acquired another one.

In fact, under US law, the Supreme Court ruled in Afroyim v. Rusk (1967) that American citizens cannot be involuntarily stripped of their citizenship. Voluntarily obtaining a second passport is explicitly not considered an expatriating act under the Immigration and Nationality Act, provided you don't formally renounce.

Similar protections exist across the UK, EU member states, and most Commonwealth nations.

The bottom line: No one is coming for your first passport because you lawfully acquired a second one.

Myth #3: "CBI Passports Are Somehow 'Less Legitimate' Than Regular Passports"

Reality: A citizenship obtained through investment is legally identical to one obtained by birth or naturalization.

When you become a citizen of St. Kitts & Nevis or Grenada through their CBI programs, you are a full citizen of that nation. You receive the same passport, the same rights, and the same international recognition as someone born on the island.

These aren't "special" passports. They aren't stamped with an asterisk. Border agents at Heathrow, Frankfurt, or Dubai don't see a CBI passport and treat it differently — because it isn't different.

In 2026, CBI programs are:

  • Regulated by sovereign governments with formal legislation
  • Subject to rigorous due diligence, often exceeding the KYC standards of major banks
  • Recognized by every international body, including the United Nations and ICAO

The St. Kitts & Nevis CBI program has been operating since 1984 — over four decades. It's one of the longest-standing and most established pathways to citizenship in the world. Grenada's program offers the added strategic benefit of access to the US E-2 Treaty Investor Visa, a feature so valuable that it alone drives significant demand.

Myth #4: "I'll Get into Tax Trouble with Two Passports"

Reality: Citizenship and tax residency are two entirely different legal concepts.

This is where the confusion gets expensive — not because dual citizenship creates tax problems, but because the fear of tax problems prevents people from making moves that could actually improve their tax position.

Here's the distinction:

  • Citizenship = your legal nationality. It's about your passport, your right to reside, and your political allegiance.
  • Tax residency = where you are considered a taxpayer. It's determined by where you live, where you spend your days, and where your economic activity is centered.

Acquiring a St. Kitts passport while living in London does not make you a St. Kitts tax resident. It doesn't change your UK tax obligations by a single penny. You're still a UK tax resident, filing in the UK, subject to HMRC rules.

However — and this is where strategic planning becomes powerful — if your life circumstances change and you relocate to a jurisdiction with favorable tax treatment, your second citizenship can provide the legal foundation to do so. This is entirely legal, entirely legitimate, and exactly what sophisticated international tax planning looks like.

The US is a notable exception due to its citizenship-based taxation model. American citizens are taxed on worldwide income regardless of where they live. But even for US citizens, a second citizenship doesn't add tax liability — it provides optionality. And for US citizens who may one day choose to formally expatriate (a significant and deeply personal decision), having a second citizenship is a prerequisite, not an afterthought.

Myth #5: "Second Citizenship Is Only for People Who Are 'Running From Something'"

Reality: The modern CBI client is running toward something — opportunity, security, freedom.

Let's retire this outdated stereotype once and for all.

The profile of the typical Meridian Advisory client in 2026 looks like this:

  • Tech founders who want frictionless access to multiple markets and investor ecosystems
  • Crypto investors seeking jurisdictions with clear, favorable digital asset regulations
  • Parents who want to give their children the gift of global optionality and access to international education
  • Frequent travelers tired of visa applications, consulate appointments, and the uncertainty of entry restrictions
  • Business owners diversifying their personal risk the same way they diversify their portfolios
  • Retirees planning for the lifestyle and healthcare options they want in their next chapter

These aren't people fleeing anything. They're planners. Strategists. People who understand that in a volatile world — where geopolitical tensions shift, policies change overnight, and borders can close with little warning (as we all learned firsthand not so long ago) — having a backup plan isn't paranoia. It's prudence.

Warren Buffett didn't become Warren Buffett by failing to plan for downside scenarios. The same logic applies to your most important asset: your freedom of movement.

Myth #6: "The Process Is Shady or Under-the-Table"

Reality: CBI programs in 2026 are among the most heavily vetted immigration pathways in the world.

If anything, it's harder to obtain citizenship through investment than through many standard immigration routes — precisely because of the enhanced scrutiny involved.

Here's what a legitimate CBI application typically involves:

✅ Comprehensive background checks conducted by independent, internationally recognized due diligence firms

✅ Source of funds verification — you must prove that your investment capital was legally earned

✅ Interpol, FBI, and international watchlist screening

✅ In-person or virtual interviews in many jurisdictions

✅ Multi-stage government review with the authority to reject any application for any reason

Rejection rates are meaningful. Not everyone who applies gets approved — and that's by design. Governments offering CBI programs have a vested interest in protecting the integrity and international reputation of their passport. One bad actor can compromise visa-free agreements that benefit the entire nation.

The Caribbean CBI programs, in particular, have undergone significant reforms in recent years, collaborating with the US, UK, and EU on enhanced due diligence standards and information-sharing agreements. The result is a process that is more transparent, more rigorous, and more credible than ever.

Myth #7: "It's Not Worth It — What Do I Really Get?"

Reality: The ROI on a second citizenship can be extraordinary — and it compounds over generations.

Let's talk tangible value.

A St. Kitts & Nevis passport provides visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to 150+ countries and territories, including the UK, EU Schengen Area, Singapore, and Hong Kong.

A Grenada passport provides similar global mobility plus eligibility for the US E-2 Treaty Investor Visa — a pathway to live and work in the United States that is unavailable to citizens of most countries.

Portugal's Golden Visa (through qualifying investment funds in 2026) provides a path to EU residency and eventually EU citizenship — unlocking the right to live, work, and study anywhere in the European Union.

Malta's citizenship program offers one of the most powerful passports on the planet, with access to 190+ destinations.

Beyond mobility, consider:

  • 📈 Business expansion: Open bank accounts, establish companies, and operate in markets previously difficult to access
  • 🛡️ Asset protection: Diversify your personal and financial risk across multiple jurisdictions
  • 🎓 Education access: Your children can study at universities across Europe, the Caribbean, or beyond — often at domestic tuition rates
  • 🏥 Healthcare options: Access public and private healthcare systems in your new country of citizenship
  • 🌍 Generational wealth: Citizenship passes to your children, and in many cases, to future generations. The investment you make today benefits your family for decades to come.

For a one-time investment — often starting at $250,000 USD for donation-based programs — you're not buying a product. You're acquiring a permanent, heritable, sovereign-backed asset with no recurring fees, no renewal requirements, and no expiration date.

Name another asset class with that profile.

So Why Does the Renunciation Myth Persist?

Three reasons:

1. Conflation of different countries' rules. Because a handful of countries (China, India, Singapore) don't allow dual citizenship, people assume the restriction is universal. It isn't.

2. Confusion between citizenship and tax residency. People hear "second citizenship" and immediately think "tax complications." As we've covered, these are separate legal frameworks.

3. Cultural stigma. In some circles, there's an unspoken assumption that "true loyalty" means holding only one passport. But in 2026, with global mobility reshaping how people live, work, and build wealth, this notion feels increasingly antiquated. Dual citizens aren't less loyal to their home country — they're simply better prepared for a complex world.

The Real Risk Isn't Getting a Second Passport — It's Not Having One

We've spent this entire article debunking reasons not to act. Let's end with the reason you should.

The world is not getting simpler. Geopolitical realignments, shifting tax policies, evolving immigration regulations, economic uncertainty, public health disruptions — these aren't hypotheticals. They're the baseline reality of modern life.

A second citizenship is the ultimate hedge. It doesn't replace your first passport — it complements it. It gives you and your family options where others have constraints. It turns "what if?" into "we're covered."

And the best time to put it in place is before you need it — because by the time you need it, it's often too late.
