Second Citizenship Strategies for SaaS Founders Planning an Exit: A 2026 Playbook

April 05, 2026 | Meridian Advisory

Why the smartest founders are structuring their global mobility before the LOI hits the table.

The Exit Is the Event. Your Citizenship Strategy Is the Infrastructure.

You've spent years building your SaaS company — grinding through product-market fit, scaling ARR past eight figures, and finally fielding serious acquisition interest. Your M&A attorney is on speed dial. Your tax advisor is modeling scenarios. Your wealth manager is ready.

But here's the question almost no one asks early enough: Where are you a citizen, and how does that single fact reshape the entire financial outcome of your exit?

In 2026, as cross-border M&A activity continues to accelerate and capital gains tax regimes tighten across North America and Europe, a growing number of SaaS founders are discovering that second citizenship isn't a lifestyle perk — it's a structural component of exit planning.

This post breaks down why, how, and when to act.

Why SaaS Founders Are Uniquely Positioned for CBI

SaaS businesses share characteristics that make their founders ideal candidates for citizenship-by-investment (CBI) strategies:

  • Location-independent revenue. Your product serves customers globally via the cloud. There's no factory to relocate.
  • High-margin, high-valuation exits. Median SaaS exit multiples in 2026 hover between 6–12x ARR for companies with strong net revenue retention. That means even a modest SaaS company can generate life-changing capital gains — and life-changing tax exposure.
  • Founder mobility. Most SaaS founders already work across time zones and travel frequently. A second passport isn't a disruption — it's an optimization of existing behavior.
  • Digital-native assets. Equity, IP, and revenue streams can be restructured across jurisdictions more fluidly than physical assets.

If you're a SaaS founder with a $20M+ valuation and an exit horizon of 12–36 months, your citizenship status deserves the same strategic attention as your cap table.

The Tax Geography Problem

Let's put numbers to this.

A U.S.-based founder selling a SaaS company for $40 million in 2026 faces a combined federal and state capital gains tax burden that can exceed 30–37%, depending on the state. In California, that number creeps toward the upper bound. In New York, it's not far behind.

That's potentially $12–$15 million paid in taxes on a single liquidity event.

The Four Strategic Pathways

Based on our work with SaaS founders at Meridian Advisory, we see four primary approaches. The right one depends on your exit timeline, family situation, and risk tolerance.

1. The Early Mover (36+ Months to Exit)

Strategy: Obtain a Caribbean citizenship (St. Kitts, Grenada, or Antigua) now. Begin establishing genuine ties and tax residency in a zero-CGT jurisdiction well before the exit process begins.

Why it works: Tax authorities scrutinize residency changes that happen suspiciously close to a liquidity event. By starting early, you build a defensible paper trail — leases, utility bills, local banking relationships, time spent in-country — that supports your new tax domicile.

Best for: Founders who know an exit is coming but aren't yet in active negotiations. This is the gold standard of planning.

Timeline: Caribbean CBI programs currently process in 3–6 months. Building genuine tax residency takes an additional 6–12 months.

2. The Dual-Track Founder (18–36 Months to Exit)

Strategy: Pursue CBI concurrently with early-stage exit preparation. Use the citizenship to unlock a tax-favorable residency, and structure the company's holding entity to align with the new jurisdiction.

Why it works: Many SaaS companies are held through Delaware C-Corps or similar structures. With proper legal guidance, founders can restructure holding companies or establish new entities in jurisdictions where their second citizenship provides access — before a buyer is at the table.

Best for: Founders who are fielding inbound interest or planning to engage an investment banker within the next 18 months.

Caution: This pathway requires close coordination between your CBI advisor, international tax counsel, and M&A team. The sequencing matters enormously.

3. The Post-Exit Optimizer (Exit Complete or Imminent)

Strategy: If the exit has already happened or is weeks away, second citizenship still offers significant value — just not primarily through capital gains optimization on this transaction.

Benefits include:

  • Wealth preservation — relocating to a jurisdiction with no wealth tax, no inheritance tax, and favorable trust structures
  • Reinvestment efficiency — deploying exit proceeds into new ventures from a tax-optimized base
  • Visa-free global access — St. Kitts and Grenada passports offer 150+ visa-free destinations, including the Schengen Area
  • Family security — a second citizenship provides a permanent Plan B in an increasingly volatile geopolitical environment

Best for: Founders who missed the pre-exit window but want to optimize for the next chapter.

4. The Grenada–U.S. Strategist

Strategy: Grenada deserves its own mention because of one unique feature — it's the only Caribbean CBI nation with a treaty relationship that grants access to the U.S. E-2 Treaty Investor Visa.

Why it matters for SaaS founders: After exiting, many founders want to maintain a presence in the U.S. without being a U.S. tax resident. A Grenadian passport allows you to apply for an E-2 visa, which provides the legal right to live and work in the U.S. without triggering U.S. tax residency in the same way a green card would.

This is a powerful combination: global tax optimization + continued U.S. access.

What About Exit Taxes?

This is the question sophisticated founders always ask — and rightfully so.

Several countries, including the United States (under IRC §877A) and Canada, impose departure taxes or deemed disposition rules on residents who relinquish tax residency. In the U.S., this applies specifically to citizens who renounce and certain long-term residents.

Key points for 2026:

  • Obtaining a second citizenship does not by itself trigger U.S. exit tax. You trigger it only by renouncing U.S. citizenship or abandoning a green card.
  • Many founders choose to hold their second citizenship without renouncing, using it as optionality rather than an immediate tax play.
  • For non-U.S. founders (Canadian, European, Australian), the calculus is different, and departure tax rules vary significantly by jurisdiction.
  • Proper structuring with international tax counsel can legally minimize or defer exit tax exposure.

The bottom line: Exit taxes are real, but they're a math problem — not a deal-breaker. In many cases, the long-term savings from relocating tax residency dramatically outweigh the one-time cost of departure.

The Investment: What Does CBI Actually Cost?

SaaS founders are used to evaluating ROI. Here's the investment breakdown for the most popular programs in 2026:

St. Kitts & Nevis

  • Donation route: Starting at $250,000 (single applicant) to the Sustainable Island State Contribution (SISC) Fund
  • Real estate route: $400,000+ in approved developments (held for 7 years)
  • Processing time: 3–6 months
  • Family inclusion: Spouse, children, dependent parents, and siblings

Grenada

  • Donation route: Starting at $235,000 (single applicant) to the National Transformation Fund
  • Real estate route: $270,000+ in approved developments
  • Processing time: 4–6 months
  • Unique advantage: E-2 treaty access to the U.S.

Portugal Golden Visa

  • Investment fund route: €500,000 into qualifying Portuguese funds
  • Processing time: 12–18 months for residency; citizenship eligibility after 5 years
  • Advantage: EU citizenship and passport at the end of the road

Malta

  • Contribution + investment model: Total outlay of approximately €700,000–€1,000,000+
  • Processing time: 14–38 months depending on the residency period chosen
  • Advantage: EU citizenship with one of the world's strongest passports

For a founder staring at a $10M+ capital gains tax bill, a $250,000–$500,000 CBI investment delivers an asymmetric return that few other financial instruments can match.
